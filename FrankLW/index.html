Frank Lloyd Wright

Frank Lloyd Wright (1867-1959) was an American architect, writer, and educator who is widely regarded as one of the greatest architects of the 20th century. He revolutionized American architecture with his innovative and organic approach, blending nature and the built environment seamlessly.

Early Life

He was born to William Carey Wright, who was a preacher and a musician, and Anna Lloyd Jones, who was a teacher whose large Welsh family had settled in the valley area near Spring Green, Wisconsin. His early childhood was nomadic as his father travelled from one ministry position to another in Rhode Island, Iowa, and Massachusetts, before settling in Madison, Wisconsin, in 1878.

Wright’s parents divorced in 1885, making already challenging financial circumstances even more challenging. Therefore, to help support his family, 18-year-old Frank Lloyd Wright worked for the dean of the University of Wisconsin’s Department of Engineering while also studying at the university. Regardless, he knew he wanted to be an architect.

Life in Chicago

Wright departed Madison in early 1887, heading to Chicago where he secured employment with J.L. Silsbee, focusing on architectural detailing. Silsbee's exceptional sketching abilities inspired Wright to refine his skills in creating fluid lines and impactful accents.

Later, Wright found more fulfilling opportunities at the prestigious architectural firm of Dankmar Adler and Louis Sullivan. Rapidly advancing, Wright became Sullivan's chief assistant, and in June 1889, he married Catherine Tobin. He continued working alongside Sullivan until 1893 when he established his own architectural practice. Over time, his family expanded to include six children, and his firm flourished, employing up to 10 assistants at its peak.
