Architectural Style & Design

Prairie School

The Prairie style, which was popular in early 20th-century America, is marked by horizontal lines, open layouts, and natural materials. It was developed by Frank Lloyd Wright, and influenced by Arts and Crafts, and emphasizes harmony with the environment.

Wright's Prairie homes utilize low-pitched roofs and ample windows to blend with nature, as well as prioritize openness and light. Their impact on American architecture is profound, inspiring architects and homeowners alike with their beauty and simplicity.

Usonia

Wright used "Usonia" to represent the United States, embodying his urban planning and architectural ideals. Originating around the turn of the 20th century, the Usonian style gained traction in the 1930s.

These homes, stemming from the original Usonian house, were built with concrete blocks to reduce costs. Wright's aim was to empower homeowners to create their own blocks and construct their homes, thus saving money. The term 'Usonian Automatic' described Wright's innovative concrete building system.

Organic Architecture

The Organic Architecture was developed by Frank Lloyd Wright, and he believed architecture should study and honour nature, and be inspired by it. "We do not live in nature, we live in it", he stated. Thus, our buildings should be built in, not on, nature.

The architecture involves a respect for the properties of the materials. Using local materials and integrating what was already there, Wright created living spaces in which nature was amplified rather than ignored and added to the beauty of the landscape.
